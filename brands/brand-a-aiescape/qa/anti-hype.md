# Brand A — Anti-hype controls

> Brand A wins by *not* being one more AI thought-leader feed. This is the
> short list of things we cut at QA.

## The "specific or out" test

For every paragraph: if we removed every adjective, would there still be a
claim? If not, the paragraph is hype filler. Either add a number / name /
date, or cut it.

Examples:

| Before (hype) | After (specific) |
|---|---|
| AI dramatically improves productivity. | Claude Code shaved ~14 minutes off my mean PR-review time over 6 PRs in a week — call it 90 minutes back. |
| The future of work is AI. | Three things in my week are AI-augmented now that weren't a year ago: PR review, meeting notes, alt-text. The other 80% is still me. |
| Unlock new productivity gains. | The newsletter draft used to take 90 minutes. It's 45 now. The next 15 are the hard ones. |

## Banned phrases (extends `voice.md`)

- "In today's fast-paced world…"
- "Game-changer", "revolutionary", "ultimate", "seamless", "synergy".
- "Unlock", "leverage" (as a verb), "elevate", "supercharge".
- "AI-powered" used as filler. Of course it's AI-powered; that's the
  whole brand.
- "Studies show" / "research shows" without a link.
- "Imagine if…" — Brand A shows, doesn't ask the reader to imagine.

## Adjective-stacking

- Two consecutive adjectives modifying the same noun → cut one.
- Three or more → cut all of them and use a verb instead.

## False scarcity / urgency

- No "now or never", "limited time", artificial countdowns.
- No "secret X" — if you know it, it's not secret.
- No "only A-players know this" — it's not a tribe.

## Tone red flags

- Second-person command without earned authority ("you must", "you need
  to"). Use "I'd" or "we'd" instead, or earn the command with a worked
  example.
- Self-aggrandising name-drops. If the lesson lands without naming the
  company, name dropping is hype.
- Closing CTAs that promise transformations ("change your work forever").
  Use a single low-friction CTA instead ("if you want one of these per
  week, the newsletter is one click").

## What good looks like

- A specific Tuesday-morning anecdote.
- One number you'd defend in a meeting.
- One thing the writer dropped, plus why.
- One thing the writer would avoid, with the failure mode named.
- Nothing the writer is unsure about left in the piece unmarked.
