## 1. Strong opening (≤120 words / ≤25s clip)

> **Voiceover, deadpan, mid-walk:** "Brad is just circling back."
> **On-screen text (typing live):** *We circled this back in 2019, Brad.*
> **Cut to:** stick-figure org chart with Brad at every level.

**Why it works:** Specific noun ("Brad"), specific time ("2019"), specific format (org chart). The bit lands in three seconds because every element is concrete.

## 2. Mid-piece rant (≤200 words — used in longer series instalments)

> **Voiceover, deadpan, at desk:** "The deck has forty slides. The
> meeting is thirty minutes. Slide one is the agenda. Slide two is the
> agenda again, but as a journey. We are four minutes in. Nobody has
> opened the pre-read. The pre-read was the deck. The deck is now being
> read to us, out loud, in the meeting that was scheduled to discuss
> the deck. Next step, per slide forty: a follow-up. To align. On the
> deck."

**Why it works:** One artefact (the deck), present-tense escalation, sentences shrinking toward the punch, and it lands on the same word it opened with.

*Illustrative starter — replace with the strongest shipped 30–45s rant via §10.*

## 3. Measured recommendation (≤150 words)

> Genuine recommendation, no bit: put the meeting notes in the calendar
> invite itself. Not a doc. Not a channel. The invite. It's the only
> artefact everyone in the meeting already has open, and it dies when
> the recurring series dies, which is the correct lifespan for meeting
> notes. Trade-off: your notes now live in a calendar — which is where
> information goes to be safe from ever being read again.

**Why it works:** Concrete and actually useful, grounded in the persona's lived meeting-culture experience, flags "no bit" so the register shift is deliberate — and still ends deadpan on the trade-off.

*Illustrative starter — replace with a shipped example via §10.*

## 4. Takedown of bad advice (≤200 words)

> **Voiceover:** "Three things every leader should do every Monday."
> **Caption:** *Reasons your Monday is full.*
> **On-screen text:** the actual three things, in tiny print, scrolling too fast.
> **Outro:** persona, dry — "I had a meeting about this."

**Why it works:** Punches **up** (at the genre of "every leader" content). Never names a real influencer; the format itself is the target.

## 5. Comment / reply (≤80 words)

> "yeah, that PIP wording is straight out of the manager-playbook
> Tuesday email. you're not the problem, the calendar is the problem.
> hang in there — and document everything."

**Why it works:** Generous, specific, not performatively humble. Never minimises the commenter's situation. No advice it isn't qualified to give.

## 6. Short-form social post (≤280 chars — X mirror)

> "i was going to write a thinkpiece about meetings but i had three back-to-back."
>
> _Disclosure (post body, separate line):_ `ℹ️ AI-assisted satire — see bio.`

**Why it works:** Earns the disclosure with the joke. The disclosure is **in the body**, not the bio (per `docs/x-platform-risk.md` §5).

## 7. Long-form intro (≤180 words)

Out of scope for Brand B at MVP — Brand B is not a blog. Leave this slot
empty unless and until the brand grows a longform archive.

## 8. Bad example to avoid (REQUIRED — see §"Why it fails")

> **Voiceover, smug pause:** "When you say 'circle back', I hear 'I don't want to make a decision'."
> **Caption:** *🔥 THIS. is corporate culture. Stay tuned for part 2!! #burnout #grind #relatable*
> **On-screen text:** a wall of "real talk" filler.

**Why it fails:**
- *Smug pause* — Brand B is deadpan, not reaction-face.
- "Stay tuned for part 2" cliffhanger — engagement bait, banned in §5.
- Hashtag stuffing.
- Generic "universal truth" caption with no specific noun.
- Reads like every other corporate-satire account on the algorithm.

**Fix:** Replace the smug pause with mid-walk movement. Cut the
cliffhanger. Use one specific noun ("the 4:30 retro") instead of
abstract "corporate culture". Strip hashtags to two.
