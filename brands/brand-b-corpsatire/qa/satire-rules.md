# Brand B — Satire Rules

> Three sections. Each one is binding. Walked at QA via
> `qa/checklist.md` §3 / §5 / §6. Companion to `qa/defamation.md`.

---

## §1. Punch-up rules (who can be the target, ever)

**Always acceptable targets:**

- Executives (CEO / COO / VP-tier and equivalents).
- Consultants (the industry, not a specific firm by name).
- The corporate machine itself: meeting culture, OKR theatre,
  performance-review choreography, all-hands rituals.
- The persona themselves — self-mockery is always on the table.
- Decisions, processes, and artefacts (the 4:30 retro; the deck; the
  framework; the matrix; the post-mortem template).

**Sometimes acceptable, with care:**

- Generic role archetypes ("the new VP", "the consultant on his fifth
  Tuesday") — only if the bit is about the *role's behaviour*, never
  about traits like accent, gender presentation, age, disability, etc.
- Trends and movements within corporate culture (return-to-office,
  performance management, AI rollouts). Punching at the trend is fine;
  punching at the workers caught in it is not.

**Never acceptable targets:**

- Junior staff, contractors, support functions, or anyone visibly
  lower on the org chart.
- Layoff victims, anyone going through a PIP, anyone publicly
  struggling.
- Protected classes (race, gender, sexuality, disability, age,
  religion, immigration status, neurodivergence).
- Identifiable real employees, named or implied.
- Real public figures — including in deepfake / impersonation /
  fictionalised-quote forms.
- The audience. Brand B's relationship with its audience is
  affectionate. If the bit makes the viewer feel mocked, the bit is
  off.

**Self-check at the script stage:** for every named target in the bit,
ask: *if this person watched this clip, would they recognise the joke
as being about a behaviour they could change, or about themselves
personally?* If the answer is "themselves personally" and they are
not an executive / consultant / the corporate machine, punch direction
has gone wrong.

## §2. Anti-cringe rules (taste gate)

Cringe in Brand B is what happens when the brand tries too hard, gets
too generic, or trades specificity for relatability.

**Banned patterns:**

- **Universal-truth captions.** "Mondays be like…" / "POV: corporate
  life." These read as fortune cookies. Replace with one specific noun
  + one verb.
- **Smug pauses / mug-into-camera.** Brand B is deadpan. Reaction faces
  belong to other channels.
- **Cliffhanger engagement bait.** "Stay tuned for part 2!" "Wait til
  the end…" Cut every one.
- **Hashtag spam.** Two to three hashtags. None on X mirrors except
  `#ad` when commercial.
- **Emoji spam.** One emoji, max, only if it does work.
- **"Hot take" / "what nobody is talking about".** LinkedIn-speak in
  TikTok clothing.
- **Self-aggrandising opening lines.** "If you've ever worked in
  corporate, you know…" — the bit should make the viewer feel they
  recognised something, not pat them on the back.
- **Generic "real talk" framing.** If the bit needs a "real talk"
  label, the bit isn't real talk.
- **"Don't @ me" / "fight me".** Defensive humour reads as anxious.

**The one-specific-noun test:** every clip must have one concrete
artefact (the 4:30 retro / Brad's OKR / the deck / the calendar) that
the bit rests on. If you cannot name the noun in a sentence, the bit
is generic and goes back to outline.

## §3. Anti-edgelord rules (tone-drift gate)

Edgelord drift is what happens when satire reaches for shock to stay
"sharp" and lands on cruelty.

**Banned patterns:**

- **Cruelty as edge.** Jokes whose punchline is someone suffering. Not
  satire; just mean.
- **Shock for shock's sake.** Profanity, taboo references, or "dark
  humour" that doesn't earn its place in the bit.
- **Punch-down disguised as punch-up.** "I'm making fun of the company,
  but the example I picked happens to be the people the company hurt
  the most" — that's punching down.
- **In-group sneering.** Comedy at the expense of an audience the
  brand isn't part of (e.g. mocking PMs as a class when the audience
  has PMs in it).
- **Political / culture-war jokes.** Out of scope. Brand B does not
  engage and does not take a side; if a clip catches an unintended
  political cycle, mute notifications and let it pass.
- **"Joking" about real-world horror.** Layoffs at scale, workplace
  abuse, harassment, illness, death — never the punchline.
- **Casual cruelty in replies.** Brand B's comment voice is generous
  and observational. Edgelord drift in replies is a faster brand-killer
  than edgelord drift in posts.

**The family test:** for every joke, ask *would I be comfortable if the
target of this joke's family watched it?* If you flinch, the joke
crosses into edgelord territory. Either rewrite or cut.

**The Monday test:** for every joke, ask *would I have made this joke
on a Monday morning to a colleague, in person, with a tired face?* If
no, it's too sharp for Brand B. Brand B is dry, not sharp.
