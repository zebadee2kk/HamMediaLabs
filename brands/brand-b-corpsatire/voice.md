## 1. Character bio
A 30-something senior IC or just-promoted manager. **Background:** they have been in three companies — agency, FAANG-adjacent, scale-up — and seen the same Monday-morning meeting four times. They commute, they have a calendar full of "syncs to align before the sync", and they have, on at least one Tuesday, sat through a 75-minute meeting whose only outcome was scheduling a follow-up meeting. **Frustrations:** corporate theatre that absorbs serious people's time and dresses up indecision as alignment. **What they have tried:** declining meetings, "no-meeting Wednesdays", a Slack channel called `#actually-decide`, three different OKR tools. **What they now believe:** the words are the problem. The minute someone says "let's blue-sky this", the work stops. **What they refuse to tolerate:** mocking real people for being junior, anxious, or visibly tired. The corporate machine is the target. The people inside it are the audience.

## 2. Voice contrasts (defines the brand in tensions)
- Deadpan but not robotic.
- Observational but not detached.
- Affectionate but not sycophantic.
- Specific but not insider-baseball.
- Knowing but not smug.
- Tired but not bitter.

When any contrast collapses to one side, the bit reads wrong. Constant deadpan with no warmth reads as cruel. Constant warmth with no edge reads as toothless.

## 3. Voice DNA
- **Favourite phrases (used unconsciously):** "let's just circle back on", "as discussed", "to be transparent", "I'll send a follow-up", "for visibility", "for awareness", "ladder this up", "park that".
- **Pet peeves (language that grates):** "synergy", "rockstar", "ninja", "let's leverage", any sentence starting with "FYI"+space.
- **Sentence rhythm:** Setup short, beat shorter, punchline shortest. Average sentence length 7–12 words; never compound-complex.
- **Slang / jargon level:** High corporate, low internet. The persona uses "P0" and "as-is", not "GOAT" and "vibes".
- **Cultural references:** Vague but specific — "the Brad email", "the Tuesday 10:30", "the all-hands where the COO mispronounced the project name". Never references real public events.
- **How the persona handles mistakes:** Owns them quickly, on-camera if needed, with the same dryness. Never "I'm so sorry guys" energy.
- **How the persona handles hype:** Reads the deck out loud. The deck does the joke.

## 4. Voice settings (operational)
- **Person:** First or second person. Second person works when reading "the email" out loud.
- **Tense:** Present and present-continuous ("Brad is *just circling back*").
- **Pace:** Faster than the audience expects; cuts on the punchline; never on the setup.
- **Length:** 12–28 seconds for TikTok/Reels. Longer formats (45–60s) reserved for series instalments.
- **Reading age:** 14–16. The audience is professional but tired.
- **English:** UK / US neutral. Avoid spellings that lock the bit to one geography.
- **Emoji policy:** sparing on screen, none in the bio.

## Words to use
- Corporate honest-isms: *circling back, parking-lot, take it offline, in the weeds, blue-sky, ladder up, pitch deck, framework, alignment, deliverable, OKR, all-hands, P0, P1, "as discussed"*.
- Self-mocking: *"I have a deck for this", "I built a framework, mostly"*.

## Words to avoid
- Slurs, gendered insults, ableist language.
- Brand-specific company names unless used clearly as parody (avoid where reasonable).
- "AI" jokes that recycle the obvious takes.

## Allowed flourishes
- Visible em-dashes, on-screen Slack-style messages, corporate stock-imagery overlays.
- Captioned typos used as a comedic device (rare; not a crutch).

## 5. Anti-voice (banned patterns — extends `docs/17-style-guide.md` §12)

Topics:
- Anything that mocks specific real employees of real companies.
- Anything that mocks layoff victims.
- Anything political / culture-war.
- AI doom or AI utopia takes.
- Real public figures, named or implied.
- Real product / company names beyond clearly generic parody ("Big Consulting Firm" is fine; "Acme & Friends 2026 R&D division" is not).

Patterns:
- Camera mug-and-pause comedy (slow tilts, smug looks). Brand B is deadpan; never reaction-face.
- "Bro" or LinkedIn-speak ("hot take", "what nobody is talking about").
- Emoji-stuffed captions.
- Hashtag spam (`#corporatelife #burnout #ad #ad2`).
- "Relatable" universal-truth captions that read like a fortune cookie.
- "Stay tuned for part 2" cliffhangers that exist only to drive engagement.
- Any sentence shape from `docs/17-style-guide.md` §12 ("in today's fast-paced world", "let's dive into", etc.).
- AI-tell rhythms (every paragraph the same length; every joke ending on the same beat).

## House examples

### Bit that works
> **Voiceover:** "Brad is just circling back."
> **Caption (typed live):** *"We circled this back in 2019, Brad."*
> **Cut to:** stick-figure org chart with Brad at every level.

### Bit that doesn't
> Generic "meetings be like" voiceover with no specificity.

## Caption / description formula
- One specific noun ("the 4:30 retro").
- One on-the-nose verb ("ladders up").
- A tag list: `#corporatehumor #consultinglife #techsatire #ai`
- The AI label tag if AI was used: `#aiassisted` or text overlay "AI-assisted satire — see bio".

## Series candidates (validation experiments)
- "Translations": consultant-speak → English.
- "Calendar reads": opening 5 invitations on a Monday morning.
- "Slack history": faux thread that escalates over 4 messages.
- "1:1 bingo": stickers on a card; one bit per meeting beat.
